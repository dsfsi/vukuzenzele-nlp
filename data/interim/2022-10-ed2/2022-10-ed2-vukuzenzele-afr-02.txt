Vuk’uzenzele4Oktober 2022 Uitgawe 2 GESONDHEIDKgaogelo LetsebeMfundo Mxolisi Ngwenya (30) van Durban, KwaZulu-Natal is een van die min swart stamselskenk ers in die land.
Die Suid-Afrikaanse Been murgregister (SABMR) sê dat meer mense van kleur as skenkers moet registreer aan gesien ŉ pasiënt ŉ veel beter kans het om ŉ gepaste skenk er in hul eie etniese groep te vind. Slegs 10% van die  78 000 geregistreerde skenk ers in die land is, volgens die register, swartmense, met 9.9% Indiërs/Asiërs en 67% blankes.
Die SABMR sê dat die meeste skenkers stamselle skenk deur middel van ŉ proses genaamd perifere bloedstamselversameling.
Hulle sê dat dieselfde stam -selle wat in jou beenmurg gevind kan word, ook in jou sirkulerende bloed teen woordig is. ŉ Nie-sjirurgiese, buite-pasiënt prosedure word uitgevoer om dié selle te versamel. Bloed word deur middel van ŉ naald in een arm getrek en vandaar beweeg dit deur ŉ masjien wat die stamselle verwy der en versamel, waarna die bloed terugvloei in jou liggaam deur ŉ ander naald in jou ander arm. Stamselle is belangrik vir die gene sing van bloedsiektes soos leukemie, beenmurggebrek of aplasie.
Ngwenya,  ŉ sportsbe stuurder by die Durban Universiteit van Tegnologie, sê hy het aanvanklik in 2012 van stamselskenking gehoor toe die SABMR  ŉ promo sieveldtog by  ŉ plaaslike kompleks aangebied het. “Ek wou nog altyd bloed skenk en is altyd gretig om te help, so ek het daarvoor gegaan. Dit was regtig maklik want hulle het net ŉ wattemonster vanuit my mond geneem en gesê dat ek ŉ oproep kan verwag.”  Die wattemonster vanuit  ŉ potensiële skenker se mond word getoets en hul beson derhede word gestoor sodat hulle gekontak kan word indien hulle as ŉ gepaste skenker vir iemand wat stam selle benodig, geïdentifiseer word.
“Ek het laasjaar ŉ oproep ontvang met die nuus dat ek ŉ perfekte skenker vir iemand was. Die proses om stamselle te skenk neem redelik lank en behels onge veer 13 verskillende toetse wat wissel van ŉ volle fisiese ondersoek (om algemene gesondheid te toets) tot bloedtoetse. Ek moes myself ook op ŉ daaglikse basis inspuit,” vertel hy. Die uitein -delike fisiese skenking neem tussen ses en agt ure.
 Meer skenkers is nodigNgwenya sê dat mense daarvan bewus gemaak moet word dat die skenkingsproses nie sjirurgie behels nie. Hy sê dat kulturele oortuigings in sommige gevalle mense keer om skenkers te word.
Die SABMR sê dat hulle tradisionele en geloofsleiers in gesprekvoering sal betrek om stamselskenking te bevor der en aan te moedig. Hulle sê dat hoe meer swartmense stamselskenkers word, hoe meer lewens gered kan word.

Globally, depression is a serious mental health disorder that affects people from all walks of life. Severe cases of depression can lead to suicide, which the World Health Organisation lists as the fourth leading cause of death for people aged between 15 and 29.
Sabelo Gumede, a psy chologist based in Durban, KwaZulu-Natal, believes that people with albinism need all the care and sup port they can get – and that starts with changing society’s understanding of what albinism is. People with this genetic condition lack melanin, which leads to a lack of colour pigmentation in the skin, hair and eyes.
They battle with their vision and are at risk of developing skin cancer.
Gumede says the main causes of mental health difficulties in people with albinism are discrimina tion and bullying. If not addressed, this can lead to psychological difficulties, such as depression, anxiety attacks and episodes of posttraumatic stress disorder.
“People living with albi nism often feel isolated, especially as a direct result of social stigmatisation. This tends to be more prevalent in townships and rural areas.” Signs of depression are isolation, poor inter-personal relationships, poor hygiene, a lack of concentration, slow ness and suicidal thoughts. Combatting  depressionGumede says people with albinism can seek help from a private psychologist, ask their local clinic for help or reach out to an organisation that assists people with depression.
Since people with albinism live within the community, just like everyone else, friends and family should keep an eye on them and ensure they get professional help if needed.
Government and nongovernmental organisations can play a crucial role in combating hate and discri mination against people with albinism through awareness and education campaigns, particularly in rural areas, because that is where violence against people with albinism is often reported.  vOFTEN ISOLATED  by their communities, many people who live with albinism suffer in silence as life becomes unbearable.
